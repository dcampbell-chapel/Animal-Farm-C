Benjamin — "The Donkey" — skeptical, withdrawn

Traits: Intelligent but fatalistic; reads, but resists action; observant and quietly compassionate in private moments.

Study note:
Benjamin's silence is interpretive — the report argues it maps to an intelligentsia that knows but does not (or cannot) act.

Moses — "The Raven" — myth & comfort

Traits: Charismatic storyteller; promises a comforting afterlife (Sugarcandy Mountain); functions as a pacifier for the other animals.

Study note:
Moses is permitted small comforts by the ruling pigs; the report frames this as an intentional tactic to deflect unrest.

Benjamin — Deep Dive

Function, evidence, and interpretive notes from the report.

Key points

  • Benjamin is literate and intelligent but chooses not to leverage that knowledge publicly.
  • He is a cautionary figure: moral clarity without mobilization does not stop systemic abuse.
  • His one compassionate act (attempt to help Boxer) reveals inner care despite public apathy.

Selected quotes (contextualized)

"Benjamin could read as well as any pig, but never exercised his faculty."
— report excerpt
"He would say that life would go on as it had always gone on — that there was nothing worth struggling about."
— commentary: fatalism as defense.

Interpretation & historical mapping

The report maps Benjamin to a segment of the intelligentsia who recognize wrongdoing but are paralyzed by skepticism, survival instincts, or fear. Their inaction can indirectly enable the rise of authoritarian power.

Moses — Deep Dive

Role, quotes, and how the ruling class leverages myth (per the report).

Key points

  • Moses sells the idea of "Sugarcandy Mountain," a heavenly reward that comforts animals.
  • Napoleon allows Moses small privileges (beer, etc.) to keep him loyal and the animals pacified.
  • The report positions Moses as the literary stand-in for organized religion used as social control.

Selected quotes

"He claimed to know of the existence of a mysterious country called Sugarcandy Mountain..."
— report excerpt
"Napoleon allowed Moses to return and granted him certain favors so that his influence could be used to keep the animals content."
— report paraphrase

Interpretation

The report argues Napoleon's tolerance of Moses shows an intentional strategy: comfort + myth reduces revolutionary energy and channels hope into passive belief.

Allegory → Historical Mapping

Concise mapping used in the report and synthesis.

  1. Benjamin → The skeptical intellectual class: aware, often resigned, insufficiently mobilized.
  2. Moses → Organized religion (e.g., Russian Orthodox Church in the allegory): used by rulers to maintain social order.
  3. Napoleon's tactics → Combination of ideological control + tolerance of pacifying institutions to maintain dominance.

Why it matters: Understanding these roles helps readers identify social dynamics where ideas + inaction produce political outcomes.
